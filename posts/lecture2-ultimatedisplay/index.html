Lecture 2: The Ultimate Display

In 1965, Ivan E. Sutherland, a pioneer in computer human interaction and virtual reality, publised ‘The Ultimate display’. A paper in which he describes a dream of an ultimate display. According to him the ultimate display would be a display using all of the user’s body - not only the arms as a computer does-, and all of its senses -not only the touch, audio and vision as nowadays display does but incorporate smell and taste-. This display would replicate reality to a point where the user won’t be able to make a difference with the “real” reality, as he explains in the quote:

The ultimate display would, of course, be a room within which the computer can control the existence of matter. A chair displayed in such a room would be good enough to sit in. Handcuffs displayed in such a room would be confining, and a bullet displayed in such a room would be fatal. With appropriate programming such a display could literally be the Wonderland into which Alice walked

– Ivan E. Sutherland

Sutherland explains that we already are familiar and know pretty well the physics in our world and can predict easily where a object is going to fall for example. However, the ultimate display would allow us to go further and apprehend phenomenas that we are not familiar with yet like interact with object with negative mass.

(Picture: Wikipedia)

Now if we consider the Reality-Virtuality Continuum of Milgram (image above), I would say that the ulimate display described by Sutherland would be towards Augmented Virtuality. Indeed, as he says “A chair displayed in such a room would be good enough to sit in”, which means for me that the reality will actually be at the game or the virtuality’s service, for the experience to be more immersive. The virtuality becomes the new reality, and the actual reality is just a tool to make this happen.

Only 3 years following the publication, he started fulfilling this dream display by creating the first virtual reality head mounted display. This display was actually projecting 3D images over the reality which made it more of a augmented reality display.
